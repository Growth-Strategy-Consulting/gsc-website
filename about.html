Meet Elena Corrao

I learned this by living it.

We grow your strategy.

Most business problems aren't as random as they look. There's almost always a reason growth stalled, or a reason a business that should be thriving feels heavier than it should. I'm the person who spots what's off, what's being missed, what could work better. That used to feel like overthinking. Now it's how I help people.

I didn't learn this from a business plan. I learned it inside real businesses, young, watching how people move, how customers decide, what builds trust, and what quietly kills momentum. That's why my help is practical and close to the ground. I find what's true, fix what isn't working, and build businesses that feel stronger, clearer, and easier to carry.

I built this by paying attention.

I started working at 16. TJ Maxx, Dunkin', then Victoria's Secret as a visual merchandiser. That's where it clicked that marketing isn't advertising. It's psychology. People could tell which displays were mine before I said a word. I'd called my obsessiveness a flaw for years. Turns out it's the gift. I can't not see the detail everyone else walks past.

Then I drove to Florida with almost nothing. Broke, in debt, sleeping in my sister's spare room. I drove Uber. I cleaned houses. I did anything honest to climb back out. I know exactly what it feels like to work yourself to the bone and still feel like you're sinking. That's why I can sit across from a worn-down owner and actually get it.

Then I walked into a little cafe. Steady business, but never quite enough to keep the lights on for good. I took the reins, and in two years we grew it past a million a year and opened two more locations. I told myself it was luck. Then I did it again. And again. Twelve turnarounds later, I stopped calling it luck. I just see what owners can't see from inside their own business.

They call me the Pivot Queen.

Here's what 18 years taught me. The businesses that die insist on staying the thing they were the day they opened. The ones that thrive read the data, see what people are actually buying, and have the nerve to pivot. That's the gift. The old playbook doesn't hit like it used to, and AI is rewriting the rules faster than anyone can keep up. I walk in, tell you what's actually working, and show you the pivot you can't see from the inside.

Take that first cafe. It opened as coffee, gelato, and chocolate, and sales were flat. They added a few food items and people bought them. The whole idea was gluten-free, and they'd found the perfect baker, so we leaned all the way in and built a full gluten-free bakery. It became the cash cow. Lines out the door on a Wednesday at 10am. Three locations. A million and a half a year out of a 700-square-foot shop.

If the owner had insisted it stay a coffee shop, he'd have had one store, one more year, then debt. Instead he was sitting on a goldmine. Someone just had to see the pivot. Every business has another level in it. The only question is whether you'll flex to reach it.
